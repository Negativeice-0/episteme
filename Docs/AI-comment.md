# Human data is not interpretable — not because the models are bad, but because humans are too damn complex

You can’t reduce salary to education. You can’t reduce housing prices to square footage. You can’t reduce career success to years of experience. Even if you *could* — which you can’t — you’d still be missing the invisible layers: psychology, privilege, timing, luck, network effects, cultural bias, emotional intelligence, negotiation skills, gender, race, geography, mental health, childhood trauma, social capital, and the thousand tiny decisions that no dataset will ever capture.

When you try to model salary based on education alone, you’re not building a model — you’re building a myth. A comforting, mathematically dressed-up fiction that says, “If you go to college, you’ll earn more.” But the data tells a different story: two people with the same degree, same GPA, same university, same major — one earns 30% more because they negotiated better, had a mentor, lived in a city with higher demand, or simply looked “more confident” in an interview. None of that is in your CSV.

Linear Regression fails not because it’s “simple” — it fails because it *pretends* the world is simple. It assumes education → salary is a straight line. But in reality, it’s a tangled, chaotic, emotionally charged, socially mediated, historically biased, context-dependent, non-linear, multi-dimensional mess. And that’s *before* you even consider that people lie on surveys, employers game the system, and salaries are often negotiated in private — meaning your “ground truth” is already corrupted.

Random Forest and XGBoost do better not because they’re “smarter,” but because they *don’t pretend to understand*. They don’t try to explain — they just *fit*. They let the data speak in whispers, not declarations. They capture the hidden interactions: that education matters more if you’re in tech, less if you’re in creative fields; that experience matters more if you’re in management, less if you’re in entry-level roles; that age matters more in some industries, less in others.

**Here’s the brutal truth:**

> You’re not wrong for wanting to understand human behavior through models.  
> You’re wrong for thinking models can *truly* understand it.

The best models don’t give you answers — they give you *questions*. They show you where your assumptions break. They reveal the gaps in your data. They force you to confront the fact that human systems are not linear, not additive, not predictable — and never will be.

So yes — your thinking is correct. Linear Regression is a lie we tell ourselves to feel in control. Modern ML models are better at *hiding* the lie — but they don’t eliminate it. The real power isn’t in the model. It’s in the *reflection*. In asking: *Why* does this model fail here? *What* is it missing? *Who* is being left out? *How* is this data biased? *What* human truth is buried beneath the numbers?

That’s where Episteme wins — not by giving you perfect predictions, but by forcing you to *think*. To question. To reflect. To admit that no model can capture the messy, beautiful, irrational, glorious complexity of being human.

And that’s not a limitation.

It’s the point.
